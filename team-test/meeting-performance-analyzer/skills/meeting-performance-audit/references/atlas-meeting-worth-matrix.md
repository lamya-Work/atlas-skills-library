# Atlas Meeting Worth Matrix

A 2×2 framing for evaluating recurring meetings. Two questions. Four verdicts.

## The matrix

|                            | Meeting **NOT** necessary | Meeting **IS** necessary |
|----------------------------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Executive **NOT** required | **KILL**                  | **DELEGATE**             |
| Executive **IS** required  | **TRANSFORM**             | **PROTECT**              |

### KILL

Eliminate the meeting entirely. Replace with an async update — a Slack post, an email digest, a shared doc. The meeting is producing nothing the executive (or anyone) couldn't get from a written summary.

**When to recommend:** the meeting has no decisions, no action items, no debate; it's a status readout that could be a Slack thread; attendance is sparse or attention is visibly absent in the transcript.

### DELEGATE

The meeting matters, but the executive doesn't. Send someone else (a direct report, the EA, a project lead) and have them brief the executive after.

**When to recommend:** the meeting is necessary for the team but the executive's role in the room is passive (they listen, they don't decide); their input could be captured asynchronously after the fact.

### TRANSFORM

The executive does need to be there, but the meeting itself isn't earning its current shape. Change the format, length, frequency, or attendee list to fit the actual job.

**When to recommend:**
- 60-min meeting that consistently wraps in 25
- weekly meeting where every other one has nothing to discuss → biweekly
- standup that's morphed into a status meeting → split into a 5-min sync + a separate decision meeting
- meeting with eight attendees where only three speak → reduce attendees

The TRANSFORM verdict should specify the proposed change concretely.

### PROTECT

Keep the meeting, give it full prep, and defend it from rescheduling pressure. These are the meetings worth the time.

**When to recommend:** strategic decisions get made in the room; the executive's presence is structurally necessary; the meeting consistently produces concrete outcomes.

## The four categorization questions

Use these to guide each verdict:

1. **What's the purpose?** If you can't state it in one sentence — flag for KILL or TRANSFORM consideration.
2. **What decisions get made here?** If none — likely KILL or DELEGATE.
3. **Does the executive need to be there, or would a 5-minute brief do?** If a brief would suffice → DELEGATE.
4. **What would happen if we cut the frequency in half?** If nothing — TRANSFORM (drop frequency).

## Reclaimable-time estimate

Per verdict, estimate weekly hours reclaimed:

- **KILL** — full meeting duration, every occurrence in the window. (e.g., 60-min weekly meeting → 1 hr/wk reclaimed.)
- **DELEGATE** — full meeting duration minus the 5-min brief. (60-min weekly → 55 min/wk reclaimed.)
- **TRANSFORM** — duration of the change. (60 min → 30 min weekly → 30 min/wk reclaimed; weekly → biweekly with same duration → half the original duration/wk reclaimed.)
- **PROTECT** — 0 reclaimable; this is intentional cost.

The brief's executive summary totals reclaimable hours across all KILL / DELEGATE / TRANSFORM verdicts in the window.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

**Don't recommend KILL for a meeting whose transcript happened to be light this week.** The Matrix is for *patterns* — single bad occurrences are not enough evidence. The audit only sees the past 7 days, so if a meeting has only one occurrence in the window and it was thin, prefer flagging it for the executive's attention rather than emitting a hard KILL verdict.

**Don't recommend DELEGATE if the executive's only role is to make a single binary decision.** That's still a decision; DELEGATE only makes sense when the listening/observing role is the dominant mode.

**Don't recommend TRANSFORM with vague language ("make it shorter").** Always specify: how much shorter, how to compress (drop a section, change format, reduce attendees).
