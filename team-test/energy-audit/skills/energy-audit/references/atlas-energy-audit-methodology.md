# Atlas Energy Audit Methodology

The methodology behind `energy-audit`. Read this before customizing the candidate ranking, automation-readiness criteria, or report structure.

## What the audit is *for*

The audit exists to surface a small number of high-confidence automation candidates from two weeks of calendar and task data, write a real plan for each, and hand the executive a finished artifact they can act on in a single review. Three things follow:

1. **Conservative output beats comprehensive output.** Five candidates on a list nobody builds are worth less than two candidates that get shipped. The audit is hard-capped at two Automation Plans per run.
2. **The plan is the deliverable.** A list of "things you could automate" without plans is a backlog. The audit always pairs every surfaced candidate with a complete Automation Plan generated by the build-automation-plan skill.
3. **The profile is the filter.** Every candidate is weighed against what the executive said in onboarding — drains, recurring patterns, quarterly priorities. Candidates that match drain or priority alignment, all else equal, rank higher. The audit does not invent its own opinions about what should be automated.

## The conservative-output rule

**At most two Automation Plans per audit run. No exceptions.**

Even when the audit identifies five candidates that could be automated, only the top one or two pass to plan generation. The rest are not surfaced anywhere in the output.

Why:

- **Builders ship.** Long lists of "could-be-automated" items get triaged once and abandoned. Short lists with high-confidence candidates get built.
- **Every plan that gets built creates a compounding return.** Two plans built in a quarter beats five plans listed and ignored.
- **Conservative output forces the audit to be honest about confidence.** If only one candidate clearly meets the readiness criteria, the audit produces one plan. If none meet the bar, the audit produces zero plans and reports that — explicitly — in the output. Fabricating a fifth-best candidate to fill a quota is a methodology failure.

When the audit is between two and three strong candidates, prefer two over three. The cap is the cap.

## The automation-readiness criteria

A candidate must pass **all five** criteria below to be eligible for plan generation. Failing one criterion drops the candidate out of consideration entirely. There is no partial credit.

### 1. Repetitive OR triggered by a clear event

Either:
- **Repetitive** — the task happened at least twice in the audit window, OR is recurring by nature (e.g., bi-weekly investor update, weekly metrics post).
- **Triggered** — the task has a clear, recognizable input event that signals "do this now" (e.g., "I just got back from a conference," "a new customer signed up," "an investor asked for an update").

Pure one-off work fails this criterion. So does work that's recurring but unpredictable — if the executive can't describe when it happens, neither can a builder.

### 2. AI can execute end-to-end OR handle a substantial chunk

The proposed automation must do meaningful work, not just "remind the executive to do it." The bar:

- The AI does the substantive work (drafting, researching, summarizing, scheduling, looking up, formatting, sending).
- A human reviews and approves before final action where stakes are high (sending a message, executing a transaction, changing a record).
- "Substantial chunk" is judged conservatively — if the AI saves the executive less than fifteen minutes per occurrence, the candidate fails this criterion.

Reminder-only automations fail. Notification-only automations fail. Anything where the AI's role is "ping the human" fails.

### 3. Clear inputs and clear outputs

The candidate must have:
- **Inputs** that are accessible (data sources the AI can read, or content the executive can hand to it).
- **Outputs** that are concrete and verifiable (a draft email, a populated spreadsheet, a CRM record, a posted update).

If inputs are vague ("the executive's general thinking on X") or outputs are vague ("better alignment in the team"), the candidate fails. Plans need fixed inputs and outputs to be buildable.

### 4. Profile alignment (drain or priority weighting)

The candidate must align with at least one signal from the executive's profile:
- **Drain alignment** — matches a drain the executive named in onboarding (highest weight).
- **Recurring-pattern alignment** — matches a "I'm always the one who deals with X" pattern from the profile.
- **Quarterly-priority alignment** — supports a stated quarterly priority.

A candidate with no profile alignment can still pass if it is unusually strong on the other four criteria, but it ranks below any candidate of comparable readiness that does align. In a tie, drain alignment wins over priority alignment, and priority alignment wins over recurring-pattern alignment.

### 5. Buildable with current AI tooling

The proposed solution must be implementable with what AI can do today:
- Read structured data (calendars, task tools, spreadsheets, databases via APIs).
- Generate text (drafts, summaries, plans, posts).
- Execute via tool calls (send messages, create records, schedule events).
- Combine these into multi-step workflows.

Things that fail this criterion:
- Solutions requiring physical actions.
- Solutions requiring judgment that depends on context the AI cannot reasonably acquire (deeply political stakeholder navigation, novel strategic decisions).
- Solutions requiring real-time human conversation as the substantive work.

When in doubt, fail the criterion. A candidate that clearly passes is worth more than five that "could probably work with the right setup."

## Short-window mode (overlap detection)

When a manual audit run lands less than 14 days after the previous successful run, the user is offered three options (look only at the new days / run the full 14 days anyway / skip). If the user picks "look only at the new days," the audit runs in **short-window mode**: the window is `[last_run + 1, today]` instead of trailing 14 days.

In short-window mode, criterion 1 (repetitive) is interpreted softly — a single observation passes ONLY if the pattern is *clearly* recurring:

- The source event is a recurring calendar event (Google Calendar / Outlook recurrence marker present)
- The Slack thread title or first message contains an explicit recurrence phrase ("every Monday," "biweekly check-in," "weekly sync")
- The meeting transcript opening references a regular cadence ("our weekly board prep")

Otherwise drop. The methodology rejects "this happened once" as repetition signal even in short-window mode.

The rest of the criteria (AI-buildable, clear inputs/outputs, profile alignment, current-tooling-buildable) apply unchanged.

Short-window mode audits often produce zero plans. That is correct behavior. The methodology does not lower the bar to keep the report from looking empty.

## Ranking and weighting

When more than two candidates pass all five criteria, rank to pick the top one or two:

1. **Drain alignment weight** — candidates matching a stated drain rank higher than candidates without drain alignment. A drain match is worth more than a priority match because drains are the highest-impact thing to remove.
2. **Frequency weight** — among drain-aligned candidates, prefer the more frequent. Once every two weeks vs once-a-week, prefer once-a-week.
3. **Priority alignment as tiebreaker** — when drain weighting and frequency weighting produce a tie, candidates supporting a quarterly priority win.
4. **Cost-of-task as tiebreaker** — when all else is equal, prefer the candidate that consumes more of the executive's time per occurrence.

After ranking, take the top one or two. If the top candidate is dramatically stronger than the second (clear drain match, high frequency, supports a priority) and the second is borderline, prefer to ship one strong plan rather than padding to two.

## Source data treatment

The audit reads up to four data sources — calendar, task tool, meeting transcripts, and selected Slack channels — depending on which the user connected at setup. Calendar and tasks are read inline by the orchestrator; meetings and Slack are split across parallel sub-agents that each return structured candidate briefs (see `sub-agent-extraction-prompt.md` for the prompt template). Composio is the validated default wiring for all four; direct vendor MCPs work too.

### Calendar (trailing 14 days)

- **Window** — trailing 14 days from the run date at 00:00 to run date at 23:59, executive's local timezone.
- **What to extract** — event title, date, time, duration, attendees, organizer, recurrence flag.
- **What to ignore** — events the executive declined, events with no attendees other than the executive (often blocks, not real meetings), events shorter than 15 minutes (usually buffers or holds).
- **Categorization for the signals breakdown** — bucket events into: leadership/strategy, operational/admin, deep work blocks, 1on1s, external meetings, internal meetings, ad hoc. Categories are not authoritative — they're for the breakdown summary, not for ranking.

### Tasks (trailing 14 days, assigned to the executive)

- **Window** — created, assigned, or completed within the trailing 14 days from the run date.
- **Filter** — only tasks where the executive is the assignee (filtered at the source query, not post-hoc).
- **What to extract** — task title, description, status, recurrence pattern if recurring, time spent if tracked.
- **What to ignore** — tasks the executive watches but doesn't own, tasks marked "blocked" or "wontfix" without any work logged.

### Meetings (trailing 14 days, sub-agent fan-out)

- **Window** — meeting transcripts dated within the trailing 14 days from the run date.
- **Source** — direct toolkit (Fathom / Granola / Fireflies) OR a transcript folder fallback (Notion DB / Drive folder), per the user's setup choice.
- **Cluster, then batch, then dispatch** — the orchestrator clusters transcripts by pattern before batching, so each sub-agent sees a coherent slice rather than a random mix. Two-tier clustering: Tier 1 groups meetings by normalized title (catches recurring meetings — e.g., "standup", "weekly product sync"); Tier 2 groups Tier-1 leftovers by category (`one_on_one`, `team_meeting`, `board_or_leadership`, `external`, `ad_hoc`). Within each cluster, batches are capped at 3 meetings per sub-agent (a 6-meeting standup cluster becomes two batches of 3). One sub-agent per batch dispatched in parallel. Each sub-agent receives the prompt template populated with its batch's transcript references, its cluster context (cluster name + total cluster size + batch position), the full energy profile, the audit window, and the mode flag — the cluster context tells the sub-agent how confident to be about recurrence patterns it observes.
- **What sub-agents return** — structured candidate briefs (`task_name`, `frequency_signal`, `time_estimate`, `profile_alignment`, `manual_workflow`, `pattern_notes`, `source: "meetings"`, `source_refs`, `evidence_strength`).
- **Working from summaries first** — sub-agents work from each transcript's summary + agenda + action items first; only dive into the full transcript if the summary doesn't reveal a clear pattern. Most platforms auto-summarize; using that summary keeps context window usage manageable when several sub-agents run in parallel.

### Slack (trailing 14 days, sub-agent fan-out)

- **Window** — messages within the trailing 14 days, in the channels selected at setup.
- **Pre-filter (orchestrator-side)** — drop bot messages; keep messages where the executive is the author or is mentioned (`@<exec>` or in a thread the exec participated in); include parent-thread context for any kept message.
- **Batch and dispatch** — one sub-agent per channel (cap of one channel per sub-agent — Slack histories can be large). Each sub-agent receives the same prompt template populated with the channel's filtered message history, the full profile, the audit window, and the mode flag.
- **What sub-agents return** — same candidate-brief shape as meetings, with `source: "slack"` and `source_refs` = message permalinks.

### When sources are missing

If a source isn't connected, its block is omitted from `.claude/energy-audit.local.md` and the audit skips it. The signals breakdown adapts — only connected sources show up. There is no minimum source count for a "valid" audit beyond the at-least-one requirement enforced at setup.

## The quarter-staleness flag

When the audit runs, compare:
- **Current calendar quarter** of the run date (Q1 = Jan–Mar, Q2 = Apr–Jun, Q3 = Jul–Sep, Q4 = Oct–Dec).
- **Quarter of `Quarterly priorities last updated`** field on the profile.

If they differ:
- Add a banner to the report header: *"⚠ Quarterly priorities were last updated in Q[X]. We're now in Q[Y]. The audit is using the older priorities to rank candidates. To improve next audit's ranking, run `refresh my energy profile` and update them."*
- Pass `quarterly_stale: true` and `last_updated_quarter: Q[X]` in the candidate brief to build-automation-plan, so each plan includes a footnote about the staleness.

The flag never auto-triggers a refresh. It surfaces the issue and lets the executive decide.

## Report structure

Every audit produces this exact structure in `[output-folder]/YYYY-MM-DD_energy-audit.md`:

```markdown
# Energy Audit — [Executive Name] — [Audit Window]

**Run date:** YYYY-MM-DD
**Audit window:** YYYY-MM-DD to YYYY-MM-DD (trailing 14 days, or shorter window if mode is `short_window`)
**Mode:** full | short_window
**Sources:** [list of connected sources only — e.g., Calendar (Google Calendar), Tasks (Notion), Meetings (Fathom), Slack (3 channels: #exec-team, #board-prep, #ops)]
**Profile completeness:** complete | partial — [list]

> ⚠ Quarterly priorities last updated Q[X]; we're now in Q[Y]. Run "refresh my energy profile" to update.
> *(banner only appears if quarter is stale)*

## Signals breakdown

For each connected source, one line summarizing volume + shape:

- *"Calendar: N events, ~Hr hours, where time concentrated"*
- *"Tasks: N assigned, M completed, K open"*
- *"Meetings: N transcripts, ~Hr hours of recorded time"*
- *"Slack: N messages across K selected channels (#chan-list)"*

Plus a 2–4 line prose summary of the period's shape — what concentrated, anything notable, anything missing.

A calendar-only audit produces something close to the original time-and-energy bucketing (events grouped by category with hours per bucket). A multi-source audit produces a broader cross-source view that's lighter on calendar-bucket detail but heavier on pattern coverage. Either is acceptable; the audit adapts to the connected sources.

## Drain alignment

For each drain the executive named in the profile, list observed signals across all connected sources that match. If a drain had no observed match, note it. Examples:

- *"Drain: 'investor updates' — 4 Slack threads (#exec-board), 2 calendar events (board prep), 1 meeting mention. No matches in tasks."*
- *"Drain: 'expense approvals' — no observed signals this period."*

If a drain has no observed match, note it explicitly. This section is the bridge between what the executive said in onboarding and what the data shows.

## Automation candidates

Summary table of every candidate the audit evaluated against the five readiness criteria — including the ones that did not make the top-two cut. The table makes the ranking transparent: the executive sees what was considered, what scored well, and what was dropped (and why).

| Candidate | Score (out of 5) | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| [Task name 1] | 5/5 | [one-line summary of which criteria it hit and why it placed where it did] |
| [Task name 2] | 5/5 | ... |
| [Task name 3] | 4/5 | ... |
| [Task name 4] | 3/5 | [why it didn't make the cut — e.g., "covered by existing meeting-prep skill"] |

End the section with: *"Selected for Automation Plans (cap: 2): [Top candidate name 1] and [Top candidate name 2]."* Or, if zero passed: *"No candidate met the bar this audit period — see Automation Plans section for the methodology note."*

## Top candidates

The summary table above is for human transparency. This section is the **structured handoff payload** that `build-automation-plan` reads from when it drafts each plan. Both sections are required — the table never replaces the briefs.

For each candidate that passes the readiness criteria (capped at two):

### Candidate 1: [Task name]

- **Source(s):** [Calendar | Tasks | Meetings | Slack — or any combination, e.g., "Meetings + Slack" when the dedup pass merged candidates from two sources]
- **Evidence strength:** strong | moderate | weak
- **Frequency:** observed N times in audit window | recurring [pattern]
- **Estimated time per occurrence:** ~Xm
- **Profile alignment:** drain match (`<drain>`) | priority match (`<priority>`) | recurring pattern (`<pattern>`)
- **Manual workflow today:** [2–4 sentence description of how the task happens manually]
- **Notes:** [any pattern data the audit noticed — "always Monday mornings," "executive procrastinates this until day-of," etc.]

### Candidate 2: [Task name]

*(same structure, only if a second candidate passed)*

## Automation Plans

*(this section is appended by build-automation-plan, one plan per candidate, in order)*

(pending)
```

The "Automation Plans" section starts with `(pending)` when the audit skill writes the file. The build-automation-plan skill replaces `(pending)` with each generated plan as it produces them.

## The handoff to build-automation-plan

After writing the report file with the candidates section populated and the Automation Plans section as `(pending)`, the audit skill invokes build-automation-plan once per top candidate. For each invocation, it passes:

- The candidate brief (everything from the candidate section above, structured)
- The energy profile (full content)
- The path to the report file (so build-automation-plan can append to it)
- The `quarterly_stale` flag and `last_updated_quarter` if applicable

The audit skill waits for build-automation-plan to complete each invocation, then verifies the report file's Automation Plans section now contains a plan with a matching candidate name. If verification fails (plan missing, file write failed), the audit halts and reports the partial state — does not send a misleading "audit complete" notification.

After all expected plans are present, the audit invokes the notification helper to send the completion message on the configured channel.

## When zero candidates pass

If no candidate passes all five criteria, the audit:

1. Writes the report file with header, signals breakdown, and drain alignment as normal.
2. Replaces the "Top candidates" and "Automation Plans" sections with a single explicit note:

> "No candidate met the automation-readiness bar this audit period. The audit prefers to surface nothing rather than pad with weak candidates. Strong candidates emerge from clear, repetitive patterns — if the next audit period shows the same patterns more clearly, plans will follow. To increase the signal, ensure the executive's task tool is being used consistently and the energy profile is up to date."

3. Skips the build-automation-plan handoff entirely.
4. Sends the notification with a "no plans this period" subject line.

A zero-plan audit is not a failure. It's the methodology working correctly when the data doesn't support a confident recommendation.

## What the audit doesn't do

- **It doesn't categorize candidates as "delegate" or "eliminate."** That's a delegation workflow, not an automation workflow. This plugin is single-purpose.
- **It doesn't measure real energy levels.** The plugin uses what the executive said in onboarding as a stated filter. Inferring energy from calendar density or meeting patterns is unreliable and out of scope.
- **It doesn't compare across audits.** Each audit is independent. If a candidate appeared in the previous audit period and didn't get built, it can appear again in the current audit period if it still meets the criteria. The plugin does not track which candidates were "presented but not built."
- **It doesn't tune itself based on feedback.** The criteria, ranking weights, and report structure are fixed by this methodology. Customization is via editing this file in a fork, not via runtime adjustment.

## Anti-patterns the audit forbids

The audit rejects reports that contain any of the following. If a draft report contains any of these, fix the report before the handoff to build-automation-plan:

- **Treating the Automation candidates summary table as a replacement for per-candidate `### Candidate N` briefs.** The table is for human transparency on what was evaluated and what was dropped. The briefs are the structured handoff payload that build-automation-plan reads from. Both are required, every audit, no exceptions.
- **Reordering or merging the six required report sections.** The order — Header, Signals breakdown, Drain alignment, Automation candidates, Top candidates, Automation Plans — is fixed. Section 4 (summary) before section 5 (briefs) is intentional: the table sets context for which two candidates the briefs detail.
- **Padding to two candidates when only one (or zero) passes.** The cap of two is a maximum, not a target. One strong plan with a clear recommendation outperforms two plans where the second is borderline.
- **Inventing pattern data the calendar or task source doesn't actually show.** Notes in the candidate brief must come from observed events. If the audit can't confirm a pattern from the data, it doesn't claim one.
