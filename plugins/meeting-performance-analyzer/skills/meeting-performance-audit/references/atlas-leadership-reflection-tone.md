# Atlas leadership reflection — tone and voice guide

The leadership reflection section of the audit brief is a piece of writing about *the executive themselves* — how they showed up across the week's meetings. Tone matters more here than anywhere else in the brief.

## The two non-negotiables

**1. Constructive, not critical.**
The reflection is read by an executive whose week is already full. They will absorb a critical tone as a personal attack. They will absorb a constructive tone as useful intel. Same observations, different reception. Constructive framing is non-optional — even when the underlying observation is unflattering.

**2. Strengths first, growth areas second.**
Every reflection opens with what the executive did well. Even in a week where the patterns are mostly negative, find the strengths and lead with them. The growth areas land harder when they're not the first thing the executive reads.

## Voice rules

- **Second-person active voice.** "You drove the decision in the [meeting] discussion" — not "The executive drove the decision," not "Sam drove the decision," not "The meeting was decisively led."
- **Concrete, not abstract.** "You held the team to 30 minutes on the [project] update" — not "You showed strong time discipline."
- **Anchored in evidence.** Each strength and growth area must reference at least one specific meeting from the week. Patterns across multiple meetings are stronger than single-meeting observations, but a single specific moment beats a vague generalization.
- **No hedging that softens to nothing.** "You might want to consider possibly thinking about..." reads as evasive. Be direct: "When the [meeting] ran long, you let it. Cutting at the 45-minute mark would have preserved the next block."

## Section structure

The reflection is one section of the brief, with two subsections:

### Strengths

3–5 bullet points. Each bullet:
- Names the strength in one phrase
- Cites the meeting(s) where it showed up
- Says one sentence about why it mattered

Example:
> **Decisive in real-time pivots.** In the [Tuesday roadmap review], when the timeline conflict surfaced, you re-sequenced the Q3 milestones in the room rather than punting to a follow-up. The team left with a new plan, not a new meeting.

### Growth areas

2–4 bullet points. Each bullet:
- Names the pattern (not a single instance, when possible)
- Cites the specific meeting(s) where it surfaced
- Suggests one concrete behavior change for next week — actionable, low-cost, specific

Example:
> **Letting status updates run long.** Both the [Monday standup] and the [Wednesday product sync] ran 10+ minutes over because the status round-robin wasn't time-boxed. Try a 90-second-per-person timer for status portions next week — leaves the back half of the meeting for discussion.

## What NOT to say

**Don't editorialize about the executive's character.**
- ✗ "You came across as disengaged in the [Wednesday meeting]."
- ✓ "Your camera was off and you didn't speak in the [Wednesday meeting] until the last 5 minutes. If you're not the decision-maker on a topic, consider declining instead of attending in observer mode."

**Don't speculate about motivation.**
- ✗ "You seemed frustrated with [direct report] in the [Friday review] — possibly because of the timeline slip."
- ✓ "You cut [direct report] off twice in the [Friday review] and re-stated their point in your own words. The team registered it; consider letting the response land before correcting."

**Don't compare the executive to a generic ideal.**
- ✗ "Strong leaders typically..."
- ✓ "When you [specific behavior] in [specific meeting], the result was [specific outcome]."

**Don't write more than 2 short paragraphs of preamble before the bullets.**
The brief is meant to be tight. The reflection is the most easily-skimmed section; if the bullets are good, the preamble is the part that gets cut.

## Calibration

If a reflection makes the executive feel attacked, it failed the brief. If a reflection makes the executive feel like the brief saw something they hadn't quite named themselves, it succeeded.
